**What to do when something goes wrong**

### What This Policy Covers

This policy explains what to do when you have a security incident - things like lost phones, suspected hacking, phishing emails, data breaches, ransomware, or any other security problem.

### Why You Need This

When something goes wrong, people panic. This policy gives clear steps so everyone knows exactly what to do, who to contact, and how to fix it quickly.

**Without a plan:** Chaos, poor decisions, delays that make things worse  
**With a plan:** Fast response, contained damage, lessons learned

### What Counts as an Incident

**Definite incidents:**
- Lost or stolen device with customer data
- Email account compromised
- Suspected ransomware or malware
- Unauthorized access to systems
- Data sent to wrong person
- Website hacked or defaced

**Suspicious but unclear:**
- Strange emails from colleagues
- Unexpected password reset requests
- Unusual system behavior
- Unknown devices on network

**When in doubt, report it.** False alarms are fine. Unreported incidents are disasters.

### 2. What Is a Security Incident?

**Security incident:** Any event that could compromise confidentiality, integrity, or availability of business data or systems.

**Examples:**
- Lost/stolen device (phone, laptop, USB drive)
- Compromised account (email, banking, system access)
- Malware infection (virus, ransomware)
- Phishing attack (clicked suspicious link, entered credentials)
- Unauthorized access (someone accessed data they shouldn't)
- Data breach (customer/employee data exposed)
- Physical security breach (office break-in, documents stolen)
- System outage affecting customer data

### 4. Immediate Response Steps

**If you discover or suspect an incident:**

### STEP 1: STOP AND CONTAIN (Immediately)

**For lost/stolen devices:**
- [ ] Report immediately to [INCIDENT COORDINATOR]
- [ ] Note what data was on the device
- [ ] Last known location
- [ ] Don't delay - call even outside work hours

**For compromised accounts:**
- [ ] Change password immediately (if you still have access)
- [ ] Report to [INCIDENT COORDINATOR]
- [ ] Don't use the account until cleared
- [ ] Check for suspicious activity (sent emails, changed settings)

**For phishing/suspicious emails:**
- [ ] Don't click links or open attachments
- [ ] Don't reply or provide information
- [ ] Forward to [INCIDENT COORDINATOR] or [SECURITY EMAIL]
- [ ] Delete email

**For malware/ransomware:**
- [ ] Disconnect device from network IMMEDIATELY (unplug ethernet, disable WiFi)
- [ ] Don't turn off device (may lose evidence)
- [ ] Photo the screen if there's a ransom message
- [ ] Report to [INCIDENT COORDINATOR]
- [ ] Don't pay ransom without professional advice

**For unauthorized access:**
- [ ] Change passwords immediately
- [ ] Enable MFA if not already active
- [ ] Report to [INCIDENT COORDINATOR]
- [ ] Log what was accessed

### STEP 2: REPORT (Within 1 hour if possible)

**Call/Text:** [INCIDENT COORDINATOR MOBILE]  
**Email:** [INCIDENT EMAIL]  
**Out of hours:** Still report - don't wait until morning

**What to report:**
- What happened (as you understand it)
- When it happened
- What systems/data affected
- What actions you've taken
- Your contact details

**Don't worry about:**
- Whether you're overreacting (better safe than sorry)
- Getting in trouble (mistakes happen, hiding them makes it worse)
- Looking stupid (better to report false alarm than miss real incident)

### STEP 3: PRESERVE EVIDENCE (Don't destroy anything)

**Do:**
- Take photos/screenshots if safe to do so
- Note times and what you observed
- Keep emails/messages related to incident
- Write down what happened while fresh in mind

**Don't:**
- Delete anything
- "Clean up" systems yourself
- Reformat devices
- Try to fix it before reporting

### 5. Incident Coordinator Response

**When incident reported, coordinator will:**

### 5.1 Initial Assessment (Within 2 hours)
- [ ] Verify incident details
- [ ] Assess severity (Low/Medium/High/Critical)
- [ ] Activate response team if needed
- [ ] Begin incident log

### 5.2 Containment (Immediately for High/Critical)
- [ ] Isolate affected systems
- [ ] Revoke compromised access
- [ ] Remote wipe lost devices (if not recovered within 24 hours)
- [ ] Block suspicious email senders
- [ ] Disconnect compromised devices from network
- [ ] Change shared passwords if compromised

### 5.3 Investigation (Within 24 hours)
- [ ] Determine scope of incident
- [ ] Identify what data affected
- [ ] How many people/customers affected
- [ ] How breach occurred
- [ ] Whether breach is ongoing

### 5.4 Notification (Timelines vary - see Section 7)
- [ ] Internal notification (staff who need to know)
- [ ] Customer notification (if their data affected)
- [ ] ICO notification (if required - 72 hours for data breach)
- [ ] Other authorities (police if theft/fraud, Action Fraud)

### 5.5 Recovery (ASAP)
- [ ] Remove malware/threats
- [ ] Restore from backups if needed
- [ ] Reset compromised passwords
- [ ] Rebuild compromised systems
- [ ] Verify integrity of data
- [ ] Resume normal operations

### 5.6 Post-Incident Review (Within 2 weeks)
- [ ] Document what happened
- [ ] Analyze root cause
- [ ] Identify lessons learned
- [ ] Update policies/procedures
- [ ] Additional training if needed
- [ ] Close incident

### 6. Incident Severity Levels

**Critical (Act within minutes):**
- Ransomware infection
- Active data breach in progress
- Banking/payment system compromised
- Multiple systems affected

**High (Act within 1 hour):**
- Email account compromised
- Customer data breach
- Lost device with customer data
- Unauthorized access to systems

**Medium (Act within 4 hours):**
- Suspected phishing incident
- Single device with limited data
- Minor unauthorized access
- Suspicious but unconfirmed activity

**Low (Act within 24 hours):**
- Spam/phishing email (not clicked)
- Minor policy violation
- False alarm with no impact

### 7. Breach Notification Requirements

### 7.1 When to Notify ICO (Data Breaches)

**Must notify within 72 hours if:**
- Personal data breach
- Risk to individuals' rights and freedoms
- High volume of records affected
- Sensitive data involved

**Examples requiring ICO notification:**
- Lost unencrypted phone with 100+ customer records
- Email breach exposing customer payment details
- Ransomware encrypting customer database

**May not require notification:**
- Encrypted device lost (data protected)
- Small number of records, low risk
- Data already public
- Quick containment with no access

**When in doubt:** Contact ICO for guidance (0303 123 1113)

### 7.2 When to Notify Customers

**Notify affected customers if:**
- Their data was involved in high-risk breach
- Could result in identity theft, fraud, or harm
- ICO recommends notification

**What to tell them:**
- What happened (in plain English)
- What data was affected
- What we're doing about it
- What they should do (change passwords, monitor accounts, etc.)
- How to contact us with concerns

**When to notify:** As soon as possible after confirming breach details

### 7.3 Other Notifications

**Police/Action Fraud:**
- Theft of devices
- Fraud or financial crime
- Criminal activity suspected

**Insurance company:**
- Cyber insurance policy may require notification
- Check policy terms

**Partners/suppliers:**
- If breach affects their data
- If shared systems compromised

### 8. Specific Incident Types

### 8.1 Lost or Stolen Phone/Laptop

**Immediate actions:**
1. Report to incident coordinator
2. Attempt to locate device (Find My iPhone, Find My Device)
3. Remotely lock device
4. If not recovered within 24 hours: remote wipe
5. Assess what data was on device
6. Change passwords for accounts accessed from device
7. Monitor for suspicious activity

**Prevention:** Screen locks, encryption, remote wipe capability (see Mobile Device Policy)

### 8.2 Email Account Compromise

**Signs:**
- Can't log in (password changed)
- Sent emails you didn't send
- Inbox rules you didn't create
- Password reset requests you didn't make

**Immediate actions:**
1. Change password immediately (if possible)
2. Enable MFA
3. Check sent items for suspicious emails
4. Check inbox rules and forwarding
5. Review account activity/login history
6. Notify contacts if spam sent from your account
7. Scan computer for malware

### 8.3 Phishing Email (Clicked Link or Entered Credentials)

**Immediate actions:**
1. Change password on affected account IMMEDIATELY
2. Enable MFA
3. Report to incident coordinator
4. Scan device for malware
5. Monitor account for suspicious activity
6. Check if other accounts use same password (change those too)

### 8.4 Ransomware Infection

**Immediate actions:**
1. Disconnect infected device from network
2. Don't turn off device
3. Photo ransom message
4. Call incident coordinator IMMEDIATELY
5. Don't pay ransom without professional advice
6. Isolate backup systems (ensure ransomware hasn't spread to backups)

**Recovery:**
- Restore from clean backups
- Rebuild affected systems
- Verify malware removed before reconnecting

**Don't:**
- Pay ransom immediately (data may not be recoverable even if you pay)
- Reconnect device without professional clearance

### 8.5 Unauthorized Access to Systems

**Immediate actions:**
1. Change passwords
2. Enable MFA if not already
3. Review who has access (remove unnecessary access)
4. Check for unauthorized changes
5. Review access logs if available
6. Determine how access was gained

**Prevention:** Strong passwords, MFA, least privilege access

### 9. Communication During Incidents

### 9.1 Internal Communication
- Staff need-to-know basis
- Use secure channels (not compromised systems)
- Regular updates to incident team
- Clear instructions for affected staff

### 9.2 External Communication
- Customers: Honest, clear, helpful
- ICO: Factual, timely, complete
- Media (if applicable): Through designated spokesperson only
- Social media: Don't discuss publicly until resolved

### 9.3 What NOT to Say
- Don't speculate
- Don't minimize or lie
- Don't blame individuals publicly
- Don't promise what you can't deliver

---

### 10. Post-Incident Activities

### 10.1 Incident Documentation
Create incident report including:
- What happened (timeline)
- What was affected
- Who was notified
- Actions taken
- Root cause
- Lessons learned
- Recommendations

**Keep for:** 3 years minimum

### 10.2 Lessons Learned Review
Within 2 weeks of incident:
- What went well
- What went poorly
- What would we do differently
- Policy/procedure changes needed
- Training needs identified
- Technical improvements needed

### 10.3 Policy and Procedure Updates
- Update policies based on lessons
- Improve technical controls
- Additional training if needed
- Test response procedures

### 10.4 Staff Communication
- Share lessons learned (anonymized if individual mistakes)
- Celebrate what went well
- Clarify new procedures
- Remind of reporting importance

---

### 11. False Alarms

**False alarms are OK.** Better to over-report than under-report.

**When someone reports false alarm:**
- Thank them for reporting
- Explain why it wasn't an incident (learning opportunity)
- Encourage continued vigilance
- No negative consequences for reporting in good faith

---

### 12. Training and Testing

**Annual:**
- Review this policy with all staff
- Test incident response (tabletop exercise)
- Update contact information
- Update procedures based on new threats

**New employees:**
- Incident reporting procedures during onboarding
- How to recognize incidents
